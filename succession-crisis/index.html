The Succession Crisis

Boomer retirement, Gen Z burnout, and the nonprofit leadership vacuum

Leaders leaving is normal. Empty seats signal trouble. The crisis begins when experienced leaders retire and nobody is ready to step in. Succession can be built before it becomes urgent, but only if leaders treat it as part of the job.

Core Framing

Succession fails when leaders wait too long to build the future inside the present.

Too many leaders are leaving before the next leader is ready. The bench is thin. The timing is late. This campaign names the problem clearly and without blame, then shows what it looks like when succession becomes part of leadership itself.

Audience Design

Three tracks, one succession problem

Each track speaks to a different relationship with the succession problem. Together they cover continuity risk, leadership exits, bench strength, and what renewal actually requires.

Gen Z Track

The mission still matters. The leadership path just looks too costly to take.

Younger nonprofit workers are not apathetic. They see the burnout, the pay, the pace, and they make a rational decision. If the path to leadership looks punishing, fewer people will choose it. This track validates that experience honestly.

  • Voice: direct, validating, hopeful
  • Message: the path needs redesign, not denial
  • Channels: short-form video, social, email, audio snippets
Messenger: Brendo should be the face here. Credibility matters.
Episode 18 · Do Well While Doing Good
"Nonprofit professionals deserve fair pay."
Gen Z workers see the compensation gap clearly. This Morganism validates that seeing it is not disloyalty.
Boomer / Legacy Leader Track

You built something worth preserving. Now the handoff deserves the same care.

Legacy leaders carry real wisdom. The risk is not that they leave; everyone retires eventually. The risk is that their knowledge walks out the door with them because nobody built the bench in time. This track speaks peer-to-peer, with respect and candor.

  • Voice: seasoned, candid, generous
  • Message: succession is a leadership act, not an administrative task
  • Channels: LinkedIn, newsletter, podcast, partner conversations
Messenger: Jim Morgan is the natural peer voice here. He can challenge without sounding accusatory.
Episode 18 (Planning) · Succession Planning
"Maintain a list of 3 potential additions."
Morgan's own succession practice: always know who could step in. The bench is not an abstraction, it is a list with names on it.
Bridge / Intergenerational Track

Neither generation caused this. Both have to build what comes next.

This is the AWNP signature position. Honest enough to name the structural failures, hopeful enough to show what rebuilding looks like. The bridge track earns credibility by refusing to flatten the problem into generational blame.

  • Voice: applied wisdom, not both-sides mush
  • Message: continuity comes from redesign
  • Channels: pillar pieces, partner briefs, talks, keynote material
Writing note: Earn trust by being specific about what is no longer sustainable. Then show what stronger leadership actually looks like in practice.
Episode 5 · Tone at the Top
"Board culture IS organizational culture."
The bridge track connects generations, but the tone is set at the top. If the board models intergenerational respect, the rest of the organization follows.
