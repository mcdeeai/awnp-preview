Frequently Asked Questions

About This Episode

What does "culture eats strategy for breakfast" mean for nonprofits?

According to Jim Morgan in Applied Wisdom for the Nonprofit Sector, this means that even the best strategic plan will fail if your organizational culture is broken. Culture is the operating system — if it's dysfunctional, no strategy will run properly. Morgan argues that culture is defined by behavior and reinforced by what leaders reward, not by mission statements or posters on the wall.

How can a nonprofit leader actually change organizational culture?

Morgan teaches that culture cannot be changed by decree, offsites, or slogans. It changes through deliberate action in three areas: structure, processes, and people. Leaders must hire people who embody the values, create systems that reward desired behaviors, and model those behaviors themselves. Morgan calls this "walking the talk."

What is "tone at the top" in nonprofit leadership?

Tone at the top is Morgan's principle that board behavior sets the absolute ceiling for staff behavior. If board members aren't respectful, transparent, and accountable, no amount of staff training will fix toxicity below. Culture flows downhill — the board's dysfunction becomes the organization's dysfunction.

What is Morgan's gap analysis technique?

Go two or three levels deep in your organization — past your direct reports — and ask frontline staff one question: "What is one thing we say we value but don't actually do?" The gap between stated values and actual behavior reveals exactly where your culture is broken.

Who is Jim Morgan?

Jim Morgan transformed Applied Materials from near-bankruptcy into a global technology giant, then dedicated himself to the nonprofit sector through the Nature Conservancy and the Northern Sierra Partnership. His book Applied Wisdom for the Nonprofit Sector distills decades of leadership experience into 66 practical concepts for nonprofit leaders. The book and all resources are available free at appliedwisdomfornonprofits.org.
