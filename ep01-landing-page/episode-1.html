About CalNonprofits

Co-Presented Partner

The Voice of California's 290,000 Nonprofits

CalNonprofits (California Association of Nonprofits) represents over 10,000 member organizations and serves as the voice of California's nonprofit sector: 290,000 nonprofits employing 1.2 million people and generating more than $200 billion in revenue.

Their Causes Count research tells the story in numbers that are hard to ignore. CalNonprofits sees the patterns across the entire ecosystem - which organizations thrive and which ones struggle, which retain their best people and which have revolving doors.

Why this episode matters for CalNonprofits: When 10,000 member organizations each improve their internal culture even incrementally, the ripple effect across California's communities is enormous. Better cultures mean less burnout, lower turnover, more institutional knowledge preserved, and more communities served effectively. Morgan's framework gives CalNonprofits' members a practical guide to building culture deliberately - not through retreats, but through structure, processes, and accountability.

Frequently Asked Questions

About This Episode

Why does Jim Morgan say culture is your core organizational asset?

Morgan argues that culture eats strategy for breakfast. Even the best strategic plan will fail if your organizational culture is broken. Culture determines whether your nonprofit is just good or becomes great. It's built through structure, processes, and people - reinforced day after day, not through slogans or retreats.

How does CalNonprofits support culture-building across California's nonprofits?

CalNonprofits represents over 10,000 member organizations and advocates for California's 290,000 nonprofits employing 1.2 million people. They see the patterns across the ecosystem and provide infrastructure, research (like their Causes Count report), and advocacy that helps nonprofits build stronger internal cultures.

Why don't retreats and slogans change nonprofit culture?

Morgan is blunt: you will never change an organization's culture just by telling employees the culture needs to change. Catchy slogans on mugs and t-shirts don't work. Culture changes through deliberate action in structure, processes, and people. An offsite might help identify what's out of alignment, but it won't create permanent change without follow-through.

What is the first step to diagnosing a broken nonprofit culture?

Morgan's technique: ask three people in your organization - not your direct reports, but people a level or two deeper - this question: "What's one thing about how we work together that we say we value but don't actually do?" Then listen. Don't defend. Don't explain. The gap between what you say and what you do is where your culture actually lives.
