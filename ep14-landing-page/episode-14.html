In the nonprofit world, there is a dirty little secret. The general public thinks everyone in that sector is holding hands and singing kumbaya. But the reality is often a knife fight for funding. Morgan and ALF argue that this scarcity mindset is literally killing the sector's impact...
Slide 7
They propose a radical shift: start treating your partner's success, or even your competitor's success, as equal to your own. The ALF model creates a ladder of awareness. You move from I to WE to all of US. If your mission is to end homelessness and the organization across the street just housed a hundred people, that is a win, period...
Slide 8
Morgan compares running an organization to river rafting. His daughter was actually a professional river guide. You have a destination, but the current is changing every second. Rocks pop up out of nowhere. If you have a rigid 50-page plan, you crash. Court sense means seeing the whole floor...
Slide 9
Face the elevator door. Think about standing in a lobby waiting for an elevator. If you're looking at your phone when the door opens, it closes and you miss it. In a nonprofit context, the elevator door opening is an opportunity. You cannot start getting ready when the door opens. You have to already be ready...
Slide 10
Morgan has a rule for this, the 10% rule. Staff and leaders should spend 10% of their work week planning, not just doing. He makes a sharp distinction between time management and priority management. If you have 20 priorities, you effectively have zero priorities...
Slide 11
Morgan claims that success is 10 percent strategy and 90 percent implementation. He has a phrase from his manufacturing days: book it and ship it. Stop polishing the product. Who's got the monkey? A monkey is a metaphor for a problem. An employee walks into your office with a problem on their shoulder. You say, let me think about that. Boom, the monkey just leaped off their shoulder onto yours. The ownership must stay with the employee...

Frequently Asked Questions

About This Episode

What is the collaborative mindset in nonprofit leadership?

According to Jim Morgan in Applied Wisdom for the Nonprofit Sector, the collaborative mindset means treating your partner's success as equal to your own. Instead of competing for funding, visibility, or credit, nonprofit leaders who adopt this mindset build genuine partnerships. Morgan argues this isn't about being nice - it's about being strategic. In a world of limited resources, collaboration is leverage.

How does the American Leadership Forum model collaboration?

ALF builds collaborative leadership through their Fellows Program, bringing together leaders from different sectors for a year-long experience designed to break down walls between business, nonprofit, government, and education. ALF teaches the journey from I to WE to ALL OF US, closely mirroring Morgan's framework.

Why do nonprofits struggle with collaboration?

Morgan identifies a scarcity mindset as the root cause. Nonprofits often compete for the same funding, duplicate work, and treat partnerships as transactions. The instinct to protect your turf can kill organizational impact. Moving past this requires what amounts to organizational ego death.

Who is Jim Morgan?

Jim Morgan led Applied Materials for nearly three decades, transforming it from near-bankruptcy into a global technology giant. He then dedicated himself to strengthening the nonprofit sector. His book Applied Wisdom for the Nonprofit Sector distills practical leadership principles into 66 concepts covering culture, planning, and implementation. The book is available free at appliedwisdomfornonprofits.org.
