Culture Is Your Core Organizational Asset

Jim Morgan on why strategy alone will never save you. Co-branded with CalNonprofits.

The Morgan Insight

Culture Eats Strategy for Breakfast

Jim Morgan doesn't mince words: "Culture eats strategy for breakfast. Culture matters. It's your core organizational asset and establishes whether your nonprofit is just good, or if it becomes great."

That's not a bumper sticker. That's a lesson forged across nearly three decades of leading Applied Materials through booms, busts, and everything in between, and then watching nonprofit after nonprofit repeat the same mistake: reaching for a new strategy when what's actually broken is the culture.

"You will never change an organization's culture just by telling employees that the culture needs to change. You must do something, take deliberate action."

Jim Morgan

Morgan has seen the ritual play out so many times he can describe it in his sleep: catchy slogans on mugs and t-shirts, an offsite is scheduled, and the topic of "culture" is suddenly on the agenda of every meeting. And then nothing changes.

This is what separates Morgan from the management gurus who toss the phrase around like confetti. He tells you what to actually do. Culture isn't a vibe. It isn't a values poster. It's built through structure, processes, and people, reinforced day after day after day.

And here's the piece most nonprofit leaders miss entirely. Morgan insists that culture starts at the very top. When board members behave courteously and respectfully toward each other and management, they set an example for staff to emulate. Culture flows downhill.

"It's every team member's job to 'walk the talk' and commit to building a great enterprise."

Jim Morgan
A Living Example

CalNonprofits and California's 290,000 Nonprofits

If any organization understands what's at stake when Morgan talks about culture, it's CalNonprofits.

Their Causes Count research tells the story in numbers that are hard to ignore: California's nonprofit sector employs over 1.2 million people, generates more than $200 billion in revenue, and touches nearly every community in the state. That's not a niche. That's an economic engine.

And the organizations driving that impact, the ones that retain their best people, attract committed donors, and sustain their programs through funding shifts, are the ones that treat culture as their core asset, exactly as Morgan prescribes.

CalNonprofits has spent decades as the voice of this sector, representing more than 10,000 member organizations. They see the patterns across the entire ecosystem. They see which organizations thrive and which ones struggle. They see the ones where staff turnover is a revolving door and the ones where people stay for decades.

Morgan's framework gives CalNonprofits' members something they rarely get: a practical, experience-based guide to building culture deliberately. Not through retreats. Not through slogans. Through structure, processes, accountability, and the daily choices that add up to either a great organization or a mediocre one.

And the scale matters. When 10,000 member organizations each improve their internal culture even incrementally, the ripple effect across California's communities is enormous. Better cultures mean less burnout. Less burnout means lower turnover. Lower turnover means more institutional knowledge preserved, more programs sustained, more communities served effectively. Morgan would call that compound interest on culture.

Why This Matters Now

Burnout Is Endemic. Turnover Is Devastating.

The best people are quietly updating their LinkedIn profiles while leadership schedules another retreat. The instinct is always the same: a new strategic plan, a new program, a reorganization. But if your culture is broken, no strategy will save you.

CalNonprofits sees this playing out across California every day. Their research shows the widening disparities within the sector: large organizations with the resources to invest in culture and talent, and smaller organizations stretched so thin that culture becomes whatever emerges by default rather than by design.

Morgan's message is that you don't need a big budget to build a great culture. You need intentionality. You need leaders who understand that every interaction either builds or erodes the culture you want.

The organizations thriving right now aren't the ones with the best strategies. They're the ones where culture is treated as the core asset Morgan says it is.

The Wisdom Runs Deep

Ancient Truths, Modern Practice

Plato

In The Republic, argued that the character and virtues of a state's leaders shape the entire society's moral fabric. You can't legislate greatness any more than you can memo your way to a healthy culture.

Aristotle

Emphasized in the Nicomachean Ethics that virtue is built through habit, through repeated practice of the behaviors you want to become second nature. That's Morgan's "deliberate action" in philosophical language.

Pat Summitt

The legendary Tennessee basketball coach built her program around explicit standards of accountability, discipline, and care. Standards applied to everyone, reinforced daily. Culture isn't inspiration. It's infrastructure.

Frances Hesselbein

One of the most respected nonprofit leaders in American history, argued that mission, values, and people practices must be tightly aligned. Values are not slogans but operational standards that shape decisions.

"Losing yourself in the group, for the good of the group -- that is teamwork."

John Wooden

The Takeaway

This week, do one Morgan-inspired thing: ask three people in your organization, not your direct reports, but people a level or two deeper, this question: "What's one thing about how we work together that we say we value but don't actually do?"

Then listen. Don't defend. Don't explain. That gap between what you say and what you do? That's where your culture actually lives. And as Morgan teaches, closing that gap requires "deliberate action," not another offsite.

If you're a CalNonprofits member, share what you learn with your peers. Culture-building isn't a solo sport, and the sector gets stronger when organizations learn from each other's honest self-assessment.

Closing Thought

Every Interaction Builds or Destroys Culture

Morgan puts it simply: "It's every team member's job to 'walk the talk' and commit to building a great enterprise." Culture isn't a department. It isn't a project. It's what you build, or what you destroy, with every interaction, every day.

California's nonprofit sector employs 1.2 million people and serves millions more. Every one of those organizations has a culture right now. The only question is whether they're building the ones they actually want. CalNonprofits has spent decades strengthening the sector from the outside through policy, advocacy, and research. Morgan shows how to strengthen it from the inside, one culture at a time.
